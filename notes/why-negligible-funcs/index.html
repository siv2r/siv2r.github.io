Repetition Amplifies Polynomial Success Rates

Say an attacker breaks a scheme with probability ϵ\epsilonϵ in a single attempt, where ϵ\epsilonϵ is some non-negligible function of the security parameter nnn (for example, ϵ=1/n2\epsilon = 1/n^2ϵ=1/n2).

If the attacker repeats the experiment qqq times independently, the probability of failing every single time is:

(1−ϵ)q(1 - \epsilon)^q(1−ϵ)q

Since (1−ϵ)(1 - \epsilon)(1−ϵ) is strictly less than 1, multiplying it by itself qqq times makes it continuously decrease. So the success probability 1−(1−ϵ)q1 - (1 - \epsilon)^q1−(1−ϵ)q keeps growing toward 1.

With a polynomial base success rate, this actually works. If ϵ=1/n2\epsilon = 1/n^2ϵ=1/n2 and the attacker runs q=n2q = n^2q=n2 experiments, the total success probability is roughly n2⋅1/n2=1n^2 \cdot 1/n^2 = 1n2⋅1/n2=1. That's a guaranteed break.

Where This Gets Confusing

So what about making ϵ\epsilonϵ really small? If the attacker has a 0.0001%0.0001\%0.0001% chance of success, the same logic still applies. Repeating the experiment enough times drives the failure probability toward zero. The math doesn't care how small ϵ\epsilonϵ is, only that (1−ϵ)<1(1 - \epsilon) < 1(1−ϵ)<1.

I couldn't see why defining a special class of "negligible" functions would change anything. Multiplying a number less than 1 by itself always decreases it. What makes negligible different?

The Asymptotic Race

The answer isn't that the failure probability stops decreasing. It does decrease. The point is that the attacker is computationally bounded and can only run polynomially many experiments.

Modern cryptography limits the base success probability to a negligible function negl(n)\text{negl}(n)negl(n), defined as a function that shrinks faster than the inverse of any positive polynomial. For any polynomial p(n)p(n)p(n), there exists NNN such that for all n>Nn > Nn>N:

negl(n)<1p(n)\text{negl}(n) < \frac{1}{p(n)}negl(n)<p(n)1​

In practice, negligible functions shrink exponentially (like 2−n2^{-n}2−n). A polynomial-time attacker is limited to q(n)q(n)q(n) repetitions where q(n)q(n)q(n) is some polynomial. Their total success probability is bounded by:

q(n)⋅negl(n)q(n) \cdot \text{negl}(n)q(n)⋅negl(n)

For example, n10⋅2−nn^{10} \cdot 2^{-n}n10⋅2−n. As nnn grows, 2−n2^{-n}2−n crushes n10n^{10}n10. The product goes to zero no matter how large the polynomial exponent is.

This is why the definition works: a polynomial ϵ\epsilonϵ (like 1/n21/n^21/n2) can be amplified to near-certainty with polynomially many tries. A negligible ϵ\epsilonϵ cannot. Polynomial repetitions can't outpace exponential shrinkage.

Why Cryptographers Prove Bounds, Not Exact Probabilities

Security proofs don't compute the exact success probability of an attacker. They prove that a worst-case ceiling is negligible, and that's enough.

The Union Bound. If an attacker has qqq tries, each with success probability negl(n)\text{negl}(n)negl(n), the probability of succeeding at least once is bounded by:

P(at least one success)≤q⋅negl(n)P(\text{at least one success}) \le q \cdot \text{negl}(n)P(at least one success)≤q⋅negl(n)

Since polynomial ×\times× negligible is still negligible, the ceiling stays negligible. That's the whole argument.

Why the bound is tight (the binomial approximation). The exact probability of at least one success in qqq independent tries is 1−(1−ϵ)q1 - (1 - \epsilon)^q1−(1−ϵ)q where ϵ=negl(n)\epsilon = \text{negl}(n)ϵ=negl(n). Expanding (1−ϵ)q(1 - \epsilon)^q(1−ϵ)q via the binomial theorem:

1−qϵ+q(q−1)2ϵ2−q(q−1)(q−2)6ϵ3+⋯1 - q\epsilon + \frac{q(q-1)}{2}\epsilon^2 - \frac{q(q-1)(q-2)}{6}\epsilon^3 + \cdots1−qϵ+2q(q−1)​ϵ2−6q(q−1)(q−2)​ϵ3+⋯

Substituting back into 1−(1−ϵ)q1 - (1 - \epsilon)^q1−(1−ϵ)q:

qϵ−q(q−1)2ϵ2+⋯q\epsilon - \frac{q(q-1)}{2}\epsilon^2 + \cdotsqϵ−2q(q−1)​ϵ2+⋯

Because ϵ\epsilonϵ is astronomically small (say 2−2562^{-256}2−256), higher-order terms like ϵ2\epsilon^2ϵ2 and ϵ3\epsilon^3ϵ3 vanish. The dominant surviving term is qϵq\epsilonqϵ. So the Union Bound q⋅negl(n)q \cdot \text{negl}(n)q⋅negl(n) isn't just a valid ceiling, it's a very tight approximation of the exact value.

Why we still prefer the bound over the exact formula. The binomial formula 1−(1−ϵ)q1 - (1 - \epsilon)^q1−(1−ϵ)q assumes every attempt is perfectly independent. Real attackers use adaptive strategies: if the first query fails, they analyze that failure to craft a better second query. When trials are dependent, the binomial math breaks down entirely. The Union Bound holds even for dependent events. No matter how the attacker correlates their queries or learns from past failures, the total success probability is trapped below q⋅negl(n)q \cdot \text{negl}(n)q⋅negl(n).
