Modular inverses exists ONLY for co-prime elements

There are two ways to compute modular inverses: Fermat's little theorem and the extended Euclidean algorithm. Fermat gives you a−1≡ap−1−1(modp)a^{-1} \equiv a^{p-1-1} \pmod{p}a−1≡ap−1−1(modp) directly, which works but requires exponentiation. The extended Euclidean algorithm doesn't use this trick at all. It just adds additional bookkeeping to the standard GCD computation to find the inverse, and it's more efficient.

But why does the extended Euclidean algorithm actually work? And why does the inverse only exist when gcd⁡(a,n)=1\gcd(a, n) = 1gcd(a,n)=1?

The Diophantine equation connection

The extended Euclidean algorithm works because there will always exist a solution for the Diophantine equation a⋅x+n⋅y=gcd⁡(a,n)a \cdot x + n \cdot y = \gcd(a, n)a⋅x+n⋅y=gcd(a,n). If we take  mod   n\bmod\;nmodn on both sides, the n⋅yn \cdot yn⋅y term vanishes and we get:

a⋅x≡gcd⁡(a,n)(modn)a \cdot x \equiv \gcd(a, n) \pmod{n}a⋅x≡gcd(a,n)(modn)

When gcd⁡(a,n)=1\gcd(a, n) = 1gcd(a,n)=1, this becomes a⋅x≡1(modn)a \cdot x \equiv 1 \pmod{n}a⋅x≡1(modn), which means xxx is the inverse of aaa in Zn\mathbb{Z}_nZn​. So the inverse exists if and only if aaa and nnn are coprime.

The time complexity is O(log⁡(min⁡(a,n)))O(\log(\min(a, n)))O(log(min(a,n))). Why not max⁡\maxmax?

Zn∗\mathbb{Z}_n^*Zn∗​ as a group

Zn∗={a∈Zn∣gcd⁡(a,n)=1}\mathbb{Z}_n^* = \{a \in \mathbb{Z}_n \mid \gcd(a, n) = 1\}Zn∗​={a∈Zn​∣gcd(a,n)=1} is the set of elements in Zn\mathbb{Z}_nZn​ that are coprime to nnn. It doesn't include 0. The size of this group is φ(n)\varphi(n)φ(n), Euler's totient function.

To verify it's actually a group, check the four axioms:

  1. Closure under multiplication. Let x,y∈Zn∗x, y \in \mathbb{Z}_n^*x,y∈Zn∗​. Since both xxx and yyy are coprime with nnn, their product is also coprime with nnn (i.e., gcd⁡(x⋅y,n)=1\gcd(x \cdot y, n) = 1gcd(x⋅y,n)=1). Now x⋅y mod n≡x⋅y−k⋅nx \cdot y \bmod n \equiv x \cdot y - k \cdot nx⋅ymodn≡x⋅y−k⋅n for some integer kkk. We need gcd⁡(x⋅y−k⋅n,  n)=1\gcd(x \cdot y - k \cdot n,\; n) = 1gcd(x⋅y−k⋅n,n)=1.
  2. Associativity. Trivial.
  3. Identity. The element 1.
  4. Inverses. Every element has an inverse because they are coprime with nnn, so a solution to the Diophantine equation exists since their gcd⁡\gcdgcd with nnn is 1.

Double-and-add is fast exponentiation in different notation

"Double and add" and fast exponentiation are basically the same algorithm. They just differ in notation.

Double-and-add computes n⋅Pn \cdot Pn⋅P: scalar multiplication is just repeated point addition. Compute P,2P,4P,…,2iPP, 2P, 4P, \ldots, 2^i PP,2P,4P,…,2iP by doubling, until 2i≤n2^i \leq n2i≤n. Add 2iP2^i P2iP to the result whenever the iii-th bit is set in nnn. This runs in O(log⁡n)O(\log n)O(logn).

Fast exponentiation computes XyX^yXy: scalar exponentiation is just repeated multiplication. Compute X,X2,X4,…,X2iX, X^2, X^4, \ldots, X^{2^i}X,X2,X4,…,X2i by squaring, until 2i≤y2^i \leq y2i≤y. Multiply X2iX^{2^i}X2i into the result whenever the iii-th bit is set in yyy. This runs in O(log⁡y)O(\log y)O(logy).
